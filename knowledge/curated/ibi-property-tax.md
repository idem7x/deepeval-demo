# IBI — Annual municipal property tax

The **Impuesto sobre Bienes Inmuebles (IBI)** is Spain's annual property
tax. It is the Spanish equivalent of council tax in the UK or property tax
in the US, and it is **paid to the municipality**, not to the autonomous
community or the central state.

## Who pays

The owner of the property as of **1 January** of each calendar year is
liable for the full year's IBI. If you buy a property mid-year, the
custom — and what most Spanish notaries write into the deed — is to
**pro-rate** IBI between seller and buyer. Spanish Supreme Court ruling
STS 409/2016 confirms that, in the absence of a contrary agreement, the
seller has the legal right to claim the buyer's share even though the
seller is technically the one on the hook.

## How the bill is calculated

IBI = *valor catastral* × *tipo de gravamen*

- **valor catastral** is the cadastral value assigned by the Catastro
  (usually significantly below market value).
- **tipo de gravamen** (rate) is set by each municipality, within the
  ranges allowed by national law: roughly 0.4 % – 1.1 % for urban
  property, 0.3 % – 0.9 % for rural property.

Big-city rates are typically toward the upper end. A €300,000 market-value
flat in Madrid with a *valor catastral* of, say, €150,000 might pay around
€600 – €1,000 in IBI per year.

## When it's billed

Municipalities issue bills once a year, usually between **August and
November**, with a roughly two-month payment window. Most owners
domiciliate it (direct debit) so it pays itself.

## Why it matters when buying

Always ask for the last paid IBI receipt before signing — it confirms the
cadastral reference of the property and any outstanding amounts. The
Property Registry can also be checked for outstanding IBI charges, which
remain attached to the property itself (*afección real*).
