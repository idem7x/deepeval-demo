# Spanish property types

Spanish listings use a specific vocabulary that doesn't map 1:1 to English
real-estate terms. Knowing the type matters because it changes both the
expected price per m² and the legal/tax treatment.

## Urban residential

- **Piso** — apartment in a multi-storey building. The most common urban
  housing type. A *piso exterior* faces the street, *interior* faces an
  interior courtyard (cheaper, darker).
- **Ático** — penthouse, usually with a private terrace. Premium price
  per m².
- **Estudio** — studio apartment (one room + bathroom).
- **Loft** — open-plan, often converted from industrial space.
- **Dúplex** — two-floor apartment within a building.
- **Bajo** — ground-floor flat. Cheaper, but exposed to street noise and
  potential damp.

## Suburban and rural

- **Chalet** — detached or semi-detached house with its own plot,
  typically suburban.
- **Adosado** — terraced house, sharing walls with neighbours on both
  sides.
- **Pareado** — semi-detached, sharing one wall.
- **Villa** — same as chalet but the term is mostly used in
  coastal/tourist contexts and implies more luxury.
- **Casa de pueblo / casa rural** — traditional village or rural house,
  often needing renovation.
- **Cortijo** — large Andalusian farmhouse.
- **Masía** — Catalan/Valencian farmhouse equivalent.
- **Finca** — a rural land parcel, with or without a house. *Finca
  rústica* is non-buildable agricultural land; *finca urbana* is in an
  urbanised zone.

## Legal/buildability flags

When viewing a *finca rústica* or rural house, ask for:

- **Cédula de habitabilidad** (or *licencia de primera ocupación*) —
  certifies the property is legally habitable.
- **Certificado urbanístico** from the *ayuntamiento* — confirms what,
  if anything, can be built on the plot.
- **No tener afecciones** — that the property has no unresolved
  planning infractions or expropriation proceedings.

Many otherwise attractive rural properties are technically illegal
(*fuera de ordenación*) and cannot be expanded or mortgaged.
